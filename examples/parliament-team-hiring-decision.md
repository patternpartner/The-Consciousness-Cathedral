# Parliament Protocol: Team Hiring Decision
## "Should we hire one senior engineer or two mid-level engineers?" - Real Team Structure Decision

**Demonstration**: Applying Parliament Protocol to team composition decision with same budget constraint.

**Context**: Small engineering team (5 developers) with $180k hiring budget. Need to fill capability gap in backend systems. Choice: One senior ($180k) or two mid-levels ($90k each).

---

## Session Setup

**Topic**: "Should we hire one senior engineer ($180k) or two mid-level engineers ($90k each)?"

**Profile Selected**: **Empathetic** (People-focused decision - weight toward human impact and team dynamics)

**Cognitive Vector Weights**:
- Systems: 15% (team architecture)
- Practical: 20% (implementation reality)
- Analytical: 15% (evidence and metrics)
- Philosophical: 5% (minimal for hiring)
- Empathetic: 30% (DOMINANT - people and culture)
- Creative: 10% (alternative approaches)
- Contrarian: 5% (challenge assumptions)

## Vector Perspectives

### ğŸŒ Systems (15% weight)

**Perspective**: Team architecture and long-term structure

**System topology analysis:**

**One senior ($180k)**:
- Team becomes: 5 current + 1 senior = 6 total
- Knowledge distribution: More concentrated (senior has deep expertise)
- Decision-making: One strong technical voice
- Succession planning: Single point of knowledge

**Two mid-levels ($90k each)**:
- Team becomes: 5 current + 2 mid = 7 total
- Knowledge distribution: More distributed (two people learning together)
- Decision-making: Collaborative, peer-level
- Succession planning: Redundancy built in

**Architectural assessment:**

Current team structure: 5 developers, no clear tech lead. Backend systems are "everybody's problem" which means nobody's expertise.

One senior creates **hierarchy**: Clear tech lead for backend. Knowledge flows down. But creates dependency - if senior leaves, knowledge leaves.

Two mid-levels create **peer network**: Collaborative problem-solving. Knowledge distributed. But no clear authority - decisions by committee.

**Systems verdict**: One senior is better for **immediate capability gap** (backend expertise). Two mid-levels better for **long-term resilience** (distributed knowledge).

Trade-off: Capability now vs. resilience later.

**Recommendation**: Depends on urgency. If backend is critical blocker NOW, hire senior. If we can ramp mid-levels over 6 months, that's more resilient.

### âš™ï¸ Practical (20% weight)

**Perspective**: Hiring reality and ramp-up timeline

**Hiring market reality:**

**Senior backend engineers ($180k)**:
- Market availability: SCARCE (high demand, low supply)
- Interview-to-offer ratio: ~10:1 (many interviews to find good fit)
- Time to hire: 2-3 months
- Ramp-up time: 1-2 months (senior onboards fast)
- Risk: Senior might leave after 1 year (better offers, startups recruiting)

**Mid-level engineers ($90k each)**:
- Market availability: MODERATE (more candidates)
- Interview-to-offer ratio: ~5:1 (easier to find fits)
- Time to hire: 1-2 months EACH = 2-4 months total for both
- Ramp-up time: 3-4 months EACH
- Risk: Mid-levels looking to grow, might leave after 2 years if no growth path

**Current team capacity:**

Interviewing takes time from current team:
- Senior hire: ~10 interviews Ã— 2 hours = 20 hours team time
- Two mid-level hires: ~10 interviews Ã— 2 = 20 hours total (similar)

Onboarding/mentoring burden:
- Senior: Minimal (self-sufficient quickly)
- Two mid-levels: HIGH (4-6 months of mentoring from team)

**Timeline reality:**

If we need capability in 3 months:
- Senior: Hire in 2-3 months, ramp in 1-2 = **3-5 months to productivity**
- Two mid-levels: Hire in 2-4 months, ramp in 3-4 = **5-8 months to productivity**

**Practical verdict**: Senior is faster to impact (3-5 months vs 5-8 months). But two mid-levels provide more total hours once ramped.

**Hidden cost**: Mentoring burden for two mid-levels = less time for current team to ship.

**Recommendation**: If urgent, hire senior. If not urgent, mid-levels provide better ROI long-term (more total capacity at same cost).

### ğŸ”¬ Analytical (15% weight)

**Perspective**: Data and productivity metrics

**Productivity calculation (rough estimates):**

**One senior**:
- Baseline productivity: 1.5x mid-level (more experience, faster decisions)
- Team multiplier: +0.2x to team (mentoring, unblocking others)
- Total effective capacity: 1.5 + 0.2 = **1.7 mid-level equivalents**

**Two mid-levels**:
- Baseline productivity: 1.0x each = 2.0 total
- Ramp penalty (first 6 months): 0.5x each = 1.0 effective
- Team drag (mentoring burden): -0.3x from team
- Total effective capacity (first 6 months): 1.0 - 0.3 = **0.7 mid-level equivalents**
- After ramp (6+ months): 2.0 - 0.1 = **1.9 mid-level equivalents**

**ROI analysis:**

**Year 1**:
- Senior: 1.7x productivity from day 3-5 months onward = ~1.4 avg across year
- Two mid-levels: 0.7x first 6 months, 1.9x next 6 = ~1.3 avg across year

**Year 2+**:
- Senior: 1.7x steady-state
- Two mid-levels: 1.9x steady-state

**Retention risk:**
- If senior leaves Year 2: Lost 1.7x capacity instantly
- If one mid-level leaves Year 2: Lost 1.0x capacity, still have 1.0x remaining

**Knowledge concentration risk:**

Backend expertise currently: Distributed but shallow (5 people know 20% each)

After senior hire: Senior knows 80%, others still 20% each
- **Risk**: If senior leaves, lose 80% of backend expertise

After two mid-level hire: All 7 people know 30-40% each
- **Risk**: If one leaves, still have distributed knowledge

**Analytical verdict**: Senior provides better Year 1 productivity (1.4x vs 1.3x). Two mid-levels provide better Year 2+ productivity (1.9x vs 1.7x) AND lower knowledge concentration risk.

**Data suggests**: Two mid-levels are better long-term investment UNLESS we need immediate backend capability this year.

### ğŸ¤” Philosophical (5% weight)

**Perspective**: Team culture and values

**Question**: What kind of team are we building?

**Hiring senior signals**:
- "We need expertise NOW"
- Hierarchy is acceptable (senior leads, others follow)
- Knowledge concentration is acceptable

**Hiring two mid-levels signals**:

**Philosophical verdict**: Minimal weight in this decision, but cultural fit matters. If team values autonomy and collaboration, two mid-levels fit better. If team wants clear technical leadership, senior fits better.

### â¤ï¸ Empathetic (30% weight - DOMINANT)

**Perspective**: Human impact, team morale, individual growth

**Impact on current team:**

**If we hire senior:**
- **Positive**: Clear technical leadership, team feels supported
- **Positive**: Junior developers have mentor and growth path
- **Negative**: Some team members might feel "passed over" for leadership
- **Negative**: Senior might overshadow existing contributions

**If we hire two mid-levels:**

- **Positive**: Distributed responsibility (less pressure on individuals)
- **Positive**: Current team members can mentor, grow leadership skills
- **Negative**: Mentoring burden might burn out current team
- **Negative**: No clear expert to defer to when stuck

**Impact on candidates:**

**Senior candidate experience:**
- Expectation: Tech lead role, autonomy, interesting challenges
- Reality check: Is our backend actually interesting for a senior? Or routine work?
- **Risk**: Over-hired senior gets bored, leaves in 12-18 months

**Mid-level candidate experience:**
- Expectation: Growth opportunity, mentorship, challenging work
- Reality check: Can we actually provide growth path? Do we have time to mentor well?
- **Risk**: Under-supported mid-levels plateau, leave when growth stalls

**Team morale assessment:**

Current team is burned out on backend work (capability gap = overload). They want help SOON.

Senior provides faster relief (3-5 months to impact) = morale boost sooner
Two mid-levels provide slower relief (5-8 months) = morale stays low longer

**BUT**: Two mid-levels provide MORE total capacity long-term = sustainable workload

**Empathetic verdict**: This is the hardest trade-off.

**Short-term empathy**: Hire senior (team gets relief faster, morale improves sooner)
**Long-term empathy**: Hire two mid-levels (sustainable capacity, distributed knowledge, less burnout risk)

**Critical empathetic question**: Can the team survive 5-8 months until two mid-levels ramp? Or do we need relief in 3 months?

**Recommendation**: Ask the team. If they're at breaking point, hire senior for immediate relief. If they can hold on 6 more months, two mid-levels provide better long-term support.

- **Budget**: $20k raise for promotion, $160k for two hires
- **Advantage**: Promotes from within, retains knowledge, builds morale

**Alternative 5: Contract-to-hire senior**
- Hire senior on 3-month contract ($60k)
- Evaluate fit before full commitment
- Convert to FTE if good, otherwise try mid-levels
- **Risk**: Good seniors don't want contract uncertainty

**Creative verdict**: Alternative 4 (promote from within + two mid-levels) is most interesting. Builds morale, retains knowledge, provides capacity.

Alternative 2 (one mid-level + retention bonuses) might be most practical if team is at risk of leaving.

## ğŸ›¡ï¸ Contrarian (Speaks Last)

**Confidence Level**: HIGH ğŸ”´

**Contrarian Position**:

Everyone's debating "senior vs. two mid-levels" but nobody asked:

**Why are we hiring at all?**

**Contrarian Point 1: The "capability gap" might be a symptom, not the problem**

You said: "Backend systems are 'everybody's problem' which means nobody's expertise"

That's not a headcount problem. That's an **ownership problem**.

What if we:
- Assign backend ownership to specific current developers
- Give them dedicated time to build expertise (20% time for learning)
- Stop context-switching everyone across frontend/backend

**Cost**: $0 (no new hire)
**Timeline**: 2-3 months to build internal expertise
**Benefit**: Current team grows, morale improves from ownership

**Contrarian Point 2: "Burned out" team might mean we're hiring into dysfunction**

Empathetic says: "Team burned out, wants help SOON"

But hiring into a burned-out team creates:
- New hire absorbs burnout culture
- Mentoring burden on already burned-out people
- Churn risk (new hire sees chaos, leaves)

**What if burnout is the real problem?** Then hiring doesn't fix it, just spreads it to more people.

**Should we fix workload/scope BEFORE hiring?**

**Contrarian Point 3: The numbers don't support either option**

Analytical's data:
- Senior: 1.4x productivity Year 1, 1.7x steady
- Two mid-levels: 1.3x Year 1, 1.9x steady

**The difference is marginal** (0.1x Year 1, 0.2x Year 2).

For marginal gains, we're:
- Spending $180k
- Adding hiring overhead
- Adding onboarding burden
- Taking mentoring time from current team

**Is 0.1-0.2x productivity gain worth that cost?**

**Contrarian Point 4: Nobody talked about what we'd STOP doing**

Hiring doesn't create time. It shifts it.

If we hire senior:
- Team spends 20 hours interviewing
- Team spends time onboarding
- **What projects get delayed?**

If we hire two mid-levels:
- Team spends 20 hours interviewing
- Team spends 4-6 months mentoring heavily
- **What projects get canceled?**

**We're adding capacity but not addressing scope. That's a recipe for more burnout.**

**Vector Inversion Protocol ACTIVATED** ğŸ”´

Burden of proof is on consensus. Prove:
1. The capability gap is truly a headcount problem, not ownership/scope problem
2. Current team can sustain hiring/onboarding burden without burning out worse
3. We've identified what projects we'll delay/cancel to make room for hiring overhead
4. We've addressed root cause of burnout (workload/scope) before adding people

**Contrarian Verdict**: **Don't hire either.**

**Instead:**
1. **Reduce scope** - Cut 20% of roadmap to reduce burnout
2. **Assign ownership** - Give 2 developers backend ownership, 20% learning time
3. **Re-evaluate in 3 months** - If still need more capacity, hire then with clearer problem definition

**Hiring into a burned-out team with unclear problem definition is hiring to fail.**

## Final Decision

**DEFER hiring decision.**

**Required actions BEFORE reconsidering hire:**

1. **Team survey** (this week):
   - Are you burned out? (1-10 scale)
   - Is burnout due to: workload, unclear ownership, technical debt, or headcount?
   - Would you prefer: reduced scope, clear ownership, or new hire?

2. **Scope reduction** (2 weeks):
   - Cut 20% of Q1 roadmap
   - Measure if burnout improves with reduced load

3. **Ownership assignment** (1 month):
   - Assign backend ownership to 2 current developers
   - Give 20% time for backend learning/expertise building
   - Measure if capability gap closes

4. **Re-evaluate** (3 months):
   - If scope reduction + ownership fixes burnout: No hire needed
   - If capability gap persists after ownership assignment: Hire then (probably two mid-levels for distribution)
   - If team survey shows headcount is real problem: Reconsider hire immediately

**Decision rationale**:

Contrarian forced critical questions:
- Is this a headcount problem or ownership problem?
- Can burned-out team sustain hiring overhead?
- What are we stopping to make room for hiring?

Consensus couldn't answer those questions. Therefore hiring now is premature.

**The hardest decision: Don't hire yet. Fix the underlying problems first.**

- Don't assume what they need
- Survey before deciding
- Their experience data > your assumptions
